## What is Soldering?

Soldering is the process of joining two or more metal components — typically electronic components to a PCB or perf board — by melting a filler metal called solder into the joint. When the solder cools and solidifies, it forms a permanent electrical and mechanical connection.

It is a fundamental skill in electronics. Without soldering, components cannot be permanently fixed to a circuit board — they would rely only on temporary connections like breadboard jumpers.

---

## Equipment Used

### Soldering Iron
The primary tool. It heats up to temperatures between 300°C and 400°C. The tip of the iron is pressed against the joint, heating both the component lead and the pad simultaneously. Solder is then fed into the joint — not onto the iron directly.

The iron must be kept clean using a wet sponge or brass wire tip cleaner. A dirty tip loses heat transfer efficiency and produces poor joints.

### Solder
A metal alloy — typically 60% tin and 40% lead (60/40) or lead-free alternatives. It comes in a thin wire form with flux core inside. The solder melts when it contacts the heated joint and flows into the connection. When it cools it forms a shiny, smooth joint.

A good solder joint is shiny and volcano-shaped. A dull, blobby joint is called a cold joint — it has poor conductivity and will fail.

### Flux
Flux is a chemical cleaning agent that removes oxidation from metal surfaces before and during soldering. Oxidation prevents solder from bonding properly. Most solder wire has flux built into the core, but additional flux paste can be applied for difficult joints or rework.

### Soldering Wick (Desoldering Braid)
A copper braid used to remove solder. You press it onto a joint you want to remove, apply the iron on top, and the braid soaks up the molten solder by capillary action. Used for correcting mistakes or removing old components.

### Helping Hands / PCB Holder
A clamp stand that holds the board steady while you solder. Essential because you need both hands — one for the iron and one for the solder wire.

### Perf Board
A perforated board with a grid of holes and copper pads. Used for building prototype circuits permanently. Components are placed through the holes and soldered to the copper pads on the underside.

---

## Safety Precautions

- Never touch the tip of the iron — it reaches 350°C and causes severe burns instantly
- Always return the iron to its stand when not in use
- Work in a ventilated area — solder fumes are harmful to breathe
- Wash hands after handling solder, especially lead-based solder
- Keep the iron away from wires and plastic components nearby

Excess component leads were trimmed with wire cutters after soldering.

The completed circuit was tested and the LED lit up correctly, confirming all joints were good.

## What Makes a Good Solder Joint

A good joint is shiny, smooth, and volcano-shaped — it flows evenly around the lead and pad. A bad joint (cold joint) looks dull, grainy, or blobby. Cold joints happen when the joint is moved before the solder solidifies, or when the surfaces weren't heated enough before solder was applied. Cold joints have high resistance and will cause intermittent failures in a circuit.

---

## Key Takeaways

Soldering is a skill that improves significantly with practice. The most important thing is to heat the joint — not the solder — and let the solder flow naturally into the connection. Clean tools, proper temperature, and patience produce reliable joints every time. This skill is directly applicable to all hardware projects — RC planes, robots, and any custom electronics build.
